Preferences for the commodification of pensions in Chile: the rol of intergenerational social mobility

Andreas Laffert1

1Institute of Sociology, Pontifical Catholic University of Chile

International Conference: The Future of Social Mobility

3-4 December 2025, Chile

Context and motivation

Context: marketisation and pensions

Privatization and commodification of public goods and social services (Ferre, 2023; Gingrich, 2011)

In Latin America, pension privatization and individual capitalization have been pivotal (Huber & Stephens, 2000; Verbič & Spruk, 2019)

Reallocate old-age risks and reshape public norms about what is fair in welfare (Borzutzky, 2012; Busemeyer & Iversen, 2020; Madero-Cabib et al., 2019)

Preferences for the market-based access to social welfare (Busemeyer, 2014; Castillo et al., 2025; Immergut & Schneider, 2020; Lindh, 2015; Svallfors, 2007)

Chile and the research gap

Chile combines high inequality, deep welfare privatisation and contested private pensions (Flores et al., 2020; Madariaga, 2020; Somma et al., 2021)

High absolute mobility coexist with short-range class moves, restricted access to elite positions, and persistent intergenerational inequalities (López-Roldán & Fachelli, 2021; Salgado et al., 2025; Torche, 2014)

We know that support for market-based services is stratified (Busemeyer & Iversen, 2020; Kerner, 2020; Lindh, 2015; Otero & Mendoza, 2024) and linked to meritocracy (Castillo et al., 2025), but less about how social mobility and meritocratic beliefs shape these preferences in the pension domain.

Research questions and H1

  • RQ1: How does intergenerational social mobility affect preferences for pension commodification in Chile?

  • Social mobility changes incomes, security and risk exposure, altering material interests in a funded DC system (Busemeyer & Iversen, 2020; Helgason & Rehm, 2023; Mau, 2015)

  • Social mobility and preferences for redistribution and social spending (Alesina et al., 2018; Ares, 2020; Gugushvili, 2017; Langsæther et al., 2022; Rodriguez & Matilla-Garcia, 2024)

  • \(H_1\): Compared to immobility, intergenerational upward mobility is associated with stronger, and downward mobility with weaker, support for the commodification of pensions

Research questions and H2

  • RQ2: How do meritocratic beliefs condition the mobility–commodification relationship?

  • Social mobility reshapes inequality attitudes by pushing the upwardly mobile toward meritocratic views and the downwardly mobile toward more structural accounts, with strong meritocratic beliefs acting as a key channel of these effects (Bucca, 2016; Deng & Wang, 2025; Gugushvili, 2016; Matamoros-Lima et al., 2025; Mijs et al., 2022; Traini et al., 2025)

  • \(H_{2}\): Under high meritocracy, upward mobility should be more pro-commodification and downward mobility less de-commodifying than under low meritocracy

Method

(M)odel, (I)nquiry, (D)ata, (A)nswer estrategy (Blair et al., 2023)

Mobility effects approach

  • I follow Breen & Ermisch (2024) to estimate causal effects of intergenerational mobility.
  • For each origin class \(O=j\), I compare movers from \(j\) to \(k\) with their counterfactual outcome if they had remained in \(j\)

Causal model (M)

Inquiry and estimand (I)

  • How does intergenerational social mobility affect individuals’ preferences for the commodification of pensions in Chile?

  • Breen & Ermisch (2024): Average causal effect of moving from origin class j to destination class k, comparing individuals who actually reach k with their counterfactual outcome had they remained in their origin j (Ecuación 1).

  • Formally, for individuals with origin \((O=j)\) who attain destination \((D=k)\), the estimand is:

\[ ATT_{j,k,j} = E\big[Y(D = k) \mid O = j, D = k \big] - E\big[Y(D = j) \mid O = j, D = k \big] \qquad(1)\]

Data (D)

  • ELSOC (COES): national urban panel 2016–2023; stratified, cluster, multistage probability sampling; target population: regular residents aged 18–75

  • I analyze 2016, 2018, and 2023 based on the availability of occupational variables

  • Analytical sample: N=3,435 observations nested in N=1,787 individuals (2016: 914; 2018: 1,377; 2023: 1,144)

  • Subsamples by mobility trajectories (e.g., low→low, low→medium), retaining movers and non-movers paired from the same class of origin

Outcome

Treatment: mobility and class matrix

  • Class assignment (O and D): Using ISCO-08/ISEI, I define origin \(O\) and destination \(D\) based on tertiles ∈ {low, medium, high}
  • For each origin \((O=j)\), the treatment is to reach \((D=k)\) (mobile) vs. immobile \((D=j)\)

Weights and covariates

  • Pre-treatment covariates \(C\): (a) parental education (highest of father/mother), in 10 ordinal categories from no schooling to postgraduate studies; (b) co-residence with both parents at age 15 (0 = no, 1 = yes); (c) nationality (0 = non-Chilean, 1 = Chilean); (d) age in years; (e) sex (0 = male, 1 = female); and (f) indigenous ethnicity (0 = no, 1 = yes).
  • Within each origin, entropy balancing reweights non-movers so their covariate distribution matches that of movers → stabilised IPW–ATT.
  • These weights target the ATT and help adjust for selection into mobility.

Heterogeneity and controls

  • Meritocracy: two items (effort and intelligence/skills rewarded), averaged and dichotomised into low vs. high meritocracy.

  • I estimate models with mobility × meritocracy interactions to study heterogeneous treatment effects.

  • The same pre-treatment covariates \(C\) enter the outcome model to block backdoor paths and achieve double robustness.

Analytic strategy (A)

  • For each origin class, I estimate weighted linear probability models:

\[ Y_{it}=\alpha+\beta T_i+X_i'\gamma+\lambda_t+\varepsilon_{it} \qquad(2)\]

  • Models use stabilised IPW–ATT weights, wave fixed effects and CR2 cluster-robust SEs by individual.
  • \(\beta\) is interpreted as \(\widehat{ATT}_{j,k,j}\): the causal effect of reaching destination \(k\) versus staying in origin \(j\)

Robustness check and sensitivity analysis

  • Unmeasured confounding (Cinelli & Hazlett, 2020): Moderate robustness in Middle→High and High→Low; borderline in High→Middle.

  • Robustness check: I re-estimate the models under three alternative codings of the outcome: (i) recoding the neutral category as agreement (3–5 vs. 1–2), (ii) excluding neutral responses from the analysis, and (iii) using the original 5-point item in a linear specification. The results are maintained and even improved (only in ii).

Discussion and conclusions

Discussion and conclusions

  1. Most Chileans reject income-based pension allocation, yet support for this market principle has grown in a highly marketised and unequal setting (Castillo et al., 2025; Lindh, 2015).

  2. Intergenerational moves into and out of upper-status positions causally shape support for pension commodification, while donward High-Middle y High-Low decrease it (Ares, 2020; Gugushvili, 2017; Rodriguez & Matilla-Garcia, 2024); meritocratic beliefs correlate with these preferences but seem to operate on a parallel track.

  3. Future work: better origin-side measures, richer batteries of market-justice items and comparative applications beyond Chile.
